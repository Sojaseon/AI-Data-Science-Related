<h2 id="toc-1"><strong>What is NLP?</strong></h2>
<p>The way humans so effortlessly work with languages might seem simple, but it&rsquo;s not. Not only can we understand the meaning of what others communicate through language, but we are also able to put our own thoughts clearly into language. In other words, our abilities are not only limited to the understanding of natural language but also expand to its generation. Therefore, the task of Natural Language processing in machines is divided into two subtasks: -</p>
<ul><li><strong>Natural Language Understanding</strong>: Techniques that aim at dealing with not only the syntactical structure of a language but also deriving semantic meaning from it come under this subtask&mdash;Speech Recognition, Named Entity Recognition, Text Classification.</li>
<li><strong>Natural Language Generation</strong>: The knowledge that is derived from NLU is taken a step further with language generation. Examples are &ndash; Question Answering, Text generation (the poem by GPT that you have read above), Speech Generation (found in virtual assistants).&nbsp;&nbsp;</li>
</ul><p>Now, <a title="NLP applications" href="https://www.projectpro.io/article/nlp-projects-ideas-/452" target="_blank" rel="noopener">NLP applications</a> like language translation, search autosuggest might seem simple from their names- but they are developed using a pipeline of some basic and simple NLP techniques.</p>

<h3 id="toc-3"><strong>1) Tokenization </strong></h3>

Most tokenizers use the &ldquo;blank space&rdquo; as a separator to form tokens. <li>White Space Tokenization</li>

<li>Penn Tree Tokenization</li>

<h3 id="toc-4"><strong>2) Stemming and Lemmatization</strong></h3>
<p>The next most important NLP technique in preprocessing pipeline that comes after tokenization is stemming or lemmatization. For example, when we are running a search on Amazon for products, say we want to see products not only for the exact word that we have typed in the search bar but also for other possible forms of the word we have typed. It&rsquo;s very likely that we will want to see product results containing the form &ldquo;shirt&rdquo; if we have entered &ldquo;shirts&rdquo; in the search box. In the English language, similar words appear differently based on the tense that they are used in and their placement in a sentence. For example, words like go, going, went are all the same words but are used based on the context of the sentence. The stemming or Lemmatization NLP technique aims to generate the root words from these variations of a word. Stemming is rather a crude heuristic process that tries to achieve the above-stated goal by chopping off the end of words, which may or may not result in a meaningful word in the end. Lemmatization on the other hand is a more sophisticated technique that aims at doing things properly with the use of a vocabulary and morphological analysis of words. By removing the inflectional endings, it returns the base or dictionary form of a word called a lemma.</p>
<p>Let&rsquo;s understand the difference between stemming and lemmatization with an example. There are many different types of stemming algorithms but for our example, we will use the Porter Stemmer suffix stripping algorithm from the NLTK library as this works best.</p>

Stemming is totally rule-based considering the fact- that we have suffixes in the English language for tenses like &ndash; &ldquo;ed&rdquo;, &ldquo;ing&rdquo;- like &ldquo;asked&rdquo;, and &ldquo;asking&rdquo;. <h3 id="toc-5"><strong>3) Stop Words Removal</strong></h3>
<p>The preprocessing step that comes right after stemming or lemmatization is stop words removal. In any language, a lot of words are just fillers and do not have any meaning attached to them. These are mostly words used to connect sentences (conjunctions- &ldquo;because&rdquo;, &ldquo;and&rdquo;,&rdquo; since&rdquo;) or used to show the relationship of a word with other words (prepositions- &ldquo;under&rdquo;, &ldquo;above&rdquo;,&rdquo; in&rdquo;, &ldquo;at&rdquo;) . These words make up most of human language and aren&rsquo;t really useful when developing an NLP model. However, stop words removal is not a definite NLP technique to implement for every model as it depends on the task. For example, when doing text classification if the text needs to be classified into different categories (genre classification, filtering spam, auto tag generation) then removing stop words from the text is helpful as the model can focus on words that define the meaning of the text in the dataset. For tasks like <a title="text summarization" href="https://www.projectpro.io/article/text-summarization-projects/693" target="_blank" rel="noopener">text summarization</a> and machine translation, stop words removal might not be needed. There are various methods to remove stop words using libraries like Genism, SpaCy, and NLTK. We will use the <a title="SpaCy" href="https://www.projectpro.io/article/spacy-projects-examples/624" target="_blank" rel="noopener">SpaCy</a> library to understand the stop words removal NLP technique. SpaCy provides a list of stop words for most languages out there. Let&rsquo;s see how to load this.</p>

<h3 id="toc-6"><strong>4) TF-IDF</strong></h3>
<p>TF-IDF is basically a statistical technique that tells how important a word is to a document in a collection of documents. The TF-IDF statistical measure is calculated by multiplying 2 distinct values- term frequency and inverse document frequency.</p>
<div style="background: #eee; border: 1px solid #ccc; padding: 5px 10px;">

<h4><strong>Term Frequency</strong></h4>
<p>This is used to calculate how frequently a word appears in a document. It is given by the following formula:</p>
<p align="center"><strong>TF (t, d) = count of t in d/ number of words in d</strong></p>
<p>The words that generally occur in documents like stop words- &ldquo;the&rdquo;, &ldquo;is&rdquo;, &ldquo;will&rdquo; are going to have a high term frequency.</p>
<h4><strong>Inverse Document Frequency</strong></h4>
<p>Before getting to Inverse Document Frequency, let&rsquo;s understand Document Frequency first. In a corpus of multiple documents, Document Frequency measures the occurrence of a word in the whole corpus of documents(N).</p>
<p align="center"><strong>DF(t)= occurrences of t in N documents</strong></p>
<p>This will be high for commonly used words in English that we talked about earlier. Inverse Document Frequency is just the opposite of Document Frequency.</p>
<p align="center"><strong>IDF(t)= N / occurrences of t in N documents</strong></p>
<p>This basically measures the usefulness of a term in our corpus. Terms very specific to a particular document will have high IDF. Terms like- biomedical, genomic, etc. will only be present in documents related to biology and will have a high IDF.</p>
<p align="center"><strong>TF-IDF = Term Frequency * Inverse Document Frequency</strong></p>
<p>The whole idea behind TF-IDF is to find important words in a document by finding those words that have a high frequency in that document but not anywhere else in the corpus. For a document related to Computer Science, these words could be &ndash; Computational, data, processor, etc. but for an astronomical document, it would be- extraterrestrial, galactic, black hole, etc. Now, let&rsquo;s understand the TF-IDF NLP technique with an example using the Scikit-learn library in Python -</p>

<h3 id="toc-7"><strong>5) Keyword Extraction</strong></h3>
<p>When you are reading a piece of text be it on your phone, newspaper, or a <a title="book" href="https://www.projectpro.io/article/popular-data-science-books-every-data-scientist-must-read/323" target="_blank" rel="noopener">book</a>, you perform this involuntary activity of skimming through it- you mostly ignore filler words and find important words from the text and everything else fits in context. Keyword Extraction does exactly the same thing as finding important keywords in a document. Keyword Extraction is a <a title="text analysis" href="https://www.projectpro.io/article/text-mining-projects/755" target="_blank" rel="noopener">text analysis</a> NLP technique for obtaining meaningful insights for a topic in a short span of time. Instead of having to go through the document, the keyword extraction technique can be used to concise the text and extract relevant keywords. The keyword Extraction technique is of great use in NLP applications where a business wants to identify the problems customers have based on the reviews or if you want to identify topics of interest from a recent news item.</p>
<p>There are several ways to do this -</p>
<ol><li>One is through TF-IDF like we have seen above. You can extract the top 10 words with the highest TF-IDF and they would be your keywords.</li>
<li>The other method that we will use for Keyword Extraction is using Gensim, an open-source Python library. This article belongs to the soc.religion.christianity category. Let&rsquo;s view the keywords now.</li>
</ol><p style="margin-left: 54.0pt;">&nbsp;</p>

</ol><h3 id="toc-8"><strong>6) Word Embeddings</strong></h3>
<p>As we know that<a title="Common Machine Learning Algorithms" href="https://www.projectpro.io/article/common-machine-learning-algorithms-for-beginners/202" target="_blank" rel="noopener"> machine learning and deep learning algorithms</a> only take numerical input, so how can we convert a block of text to numbers that can be fed to these models. When training any kind of model on text data be it classification or regression- it is a necessary condition to transform it into a numerical representation. The answer is simple, follow the word embedding approach for representing text data. This NLP technique lets you represent words with similar meanings to have a similar representation.</p>
<p>Word Embeddings also known as vectors are the&nbsp;numerical representations for words in a language. These representations are learned such that words with similar meaning would have vectors very close to each other. Individual words are represented as real-valued vectors or coordinates in a predefined vector space of n-dimensions.&nbsp;<em>This doesn&rsquo;t make much sense, does it?</em> Let's understand this with an example.</p>
<p align="center"><img style="border-width: 0px; border-style: solid; width: 100%; height: 100%;" title="What are Word Embeddings" alt="What are Word Embeddings" data-src="https://daxg39y63pxwu.cloudfront.net/images/blog/10+NLP+Techniques+Every+Data+Scientist+Should+Learn/Word+Embeddings+NLP.png"></p>
<p>Consider a 3- 3-dimensional space as represented above in a 3D plane. Each word is represented by a coordinate(x,y,z) in this space. Words that are similar in meaning would be close to each other in this 3-dimensional space.</p>
<ul><li>The distance between walked and the king would be greater than the distance between walked and walking since they have the same root word-walk.</li>
<li>Word embeddings are also useful in understanding the relationship between words- what king is to the queen, a man is to woman. Hence, in the vector space, the distance between king and queen would approximately be equal to the distance between man and woman.</li>
</ul><p>One can either use predefined Word Embeddings (trained on a huge corpus such as Wikipedia) or learn word embeddings from scratch for a custom dataset. There are many different kinds of Word Embeddings out there like <a title="Glove Word Embeddings NLP " href="https://nlp.stanford.edu/projects/glove/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">GloVe</a>, <a title="Word2Vec Word Embeddings NLP" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Word2vec" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Word2Vec</a>, TF-IDF, CountVectorizer, BERT, ELMO etc. The one we&rsquo;ll be talking about here is Word2vec.</p>
<h4><strong>Word2Vec</strong></h4>
<p>Word2Vec is a neural network model that learns word associations from a huge corpus of text. Word2vec can be trained in two ways, either by using the Common Bag of Words Model (CBOW) or the Skip Gram Model.</p>
<p style="text-align: center;"><img style="border-width: 0px; border-style: solid; width: 468px; height: 273px;" title="Word Embeddings Word2Vec" alt="Word Embeddings Word2Vec" data-src="https://daxg39y63pxwu.cloudfront.net/images/blog/10+NLP+Techniques+Every+Data+Scientist+Should+Learn/CBOW+vs+SkipGram+Word+Embeddings.png"></p>
<p align="center"><strong>Image Credit: Pathmind</strong></p>
<p>In the CBOW model, the context of each word is taken as the input and the word corresponding to the context is to be predicted as the output. Consider an example sentence- &ldquo;The day is bright and sunny.&rdquo;</p>
<p>In the above sentence, the word we are trying to predict is sunny, using the input as the average of one-hot encoded vectors of the words- &ldquo;The day is bright&rdquo;. This input after passing through the neural network is compared to the one-hot encoded vector of the target word, &ldquo;sunny&rdquo;. The loss is calculated, and this is how the context of the word &ldquo;sunny&rdquo; is learned in CBOW.</p>
<p>The Skip Gram model works just the opposite of the above approach, we send input as a one-hot encoded vector of our target word &ldquo;sunny&rdquo; and it tries to output the context of the target word. For each context vector, we get a probability distribution of V probabilities where V is the vocab size and also the size of the one-hot encoded vector in the above technique.</p>
<p>Now, let&rsquo;s see how we can implement Word2vec in python. The first step is to download Google&rsquo;s predefined <a title="Download Word2Vec File" href="https://code.google.com/archive/p/word2vec/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Word2Vec</a> file from <a title="Pre-Trained Word Embeddings" href="https://www.kaggle.com/leadbest/googlenewsvectorsnegative300/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">here</a>. The next step is to place the GoogleNews-vectors-negative300.bin file in your current directory. You can use Gensim to load this vector.</p>
<p style="text-align: center;"><img style="border-width: 0px; border-style: solid; width: 468px; height: 23px;" title="What are Word Embeddings in NLP?" alt="What are Word Embeddings in NLP?" data-src="https://daxg39y63pxwu.cloudfront.net/images/blog/10+NLP+Techniques+Every+Data+Scientist+Should+Learn/Word2Vec_Python.png"></p>
<p>This embedding is in 300 dimensions i.e. for every word in the vocabulary we have an array of 300 real values representing it. Now, we&rsquo;ll use word2vec and <a href="https://scikit-learn.org/stable/modules/metrics.html#cosine-similarity">cosine similarity</a> to calculate the distance between words like- king, queen, walked, etc.</p>
<p style="text-align: center;"><img style="border-width: 0px; border-style: solid; width: 468px; height: 51px;" alt="Word Embeddings Python" data-src="https://daxg39y63pxwu.cloudfront.net/images/blog/10+NLP+Techniques+Every+Data+Scientist+Should+Learn/WordEmbeddingsNLP_Word2VecGenismPython.png"></p>
<p>Our hypothesis about the distance between the vectors is mathematically proved here. There is less distance between queen and king than between king and walked. &nbsp;</p>

<h3 id="toc-10"><strong>8) Topic Modelling </strong></h3>
<p>Topic Modelling is a statistical NLP technique that analyzes a corpus of text documents to find the themes hidden in them. The best part is, topic modeling is an unsupervised <a title="machine learning algorithm" href="https://www.projectpro.io/article/10-awesome-machine-learning-applications-of-today/364" target="_blank" rel="noopener">machine learning algorithm</a> meaning it does not need these documents to be labeled. This technique enables us to organize and summarize electronic archives at a scale that would be impossible by human annotation. Latent Dirichlet Allocation is one of the most powerful techniques used for topic modeling. The basic intuition is that each document has multiple topics and each topic is distributed over a fixed vocabulary of words. Let&rsquo;s understand this with the help of an example.</p>
<p style="text-align: center;"><img style="border-width: 0px; border-style: solid; width: 100%; height: 100%;" title="Topic Modelling NLP Technique Python" alt="Topic Modelling NLP Technique Python" data-src="https://daxg39y63pxwu.cloudfront.net/images/blog/10+NLP+Techniques+Every+Data+Scientist+Should+Learn/TopicModelling_NLPTechnique.png"></p>

</ul><h3 id="toc-11"><strong>9) Text Summarization</strong></h3>
<p>This NLP technique is used to concisely and briefly summarize a text in a fluent and coherent manner. Summarization is useful to extract useful information from documents without having to read word to word. This process is very time-consuming if done by a human, automatic text summarization reduces the time radically.</p>
<p>There are two types of text summarization techniques.</p>
<ul><li><strong>Extraction Based Summarization</strong>: In this technique, some key phrases and words in the document are pulled to make the summary. No changes to the original text are made.</li>
</ul><p style="margin-left: 72.0pt;" align="center"><img style="border-width: 0px; border-style: solid; width: 500px; height: 300px;" title="Extraction Based Text Summarization" alt="Extraction Based Text Summarization" data-src="https://daxg39y63pxwu.cloudfront.net/images/blog/10+NLP+Techniques+Every+Data+Scientist+Should+Learn/Extraction+Based+Text+Summarization+NLP+Technique.png"></p>
<ul><li><strong>Abstraction Based Summarization</strong>: In this text summarization technique, new phrases and sentences are created from the original document that captures the most useful information. The language and sentence structure of the summary is not the same as the original document because this technique involves paraphrasing. We can also overcome the grammatical inconsistencies found in extraction-based methods.</li>
</ul><p style="margin-left: 72.0pt;">&nbsp;</p>
<p style="margin-left: 72.0pt;" align="center"><img style="border-width: 0px; border-style: solid; width: 500px; height: 300px;" title="Abstraction Based Text Summarization" alt="Abstraction Based Text Summarization" data-src="https://daxg39y63pxwu.cloudfront.net/images/blog/10+NLP+Techniques+Every+Data+Scientist+Should+Learn/Abstraction+Based+Text+Summarization+NLP+Technique.png"></p>

<p>The next step is to tokenize the document and remove stop words and punctuations. After that, we&rsquo;ll use a counter to count the frequency of words and get the top-5 most frequent words in the document.</p>

<h3 id="toc-12"><strong>10) Named Entity Recognition</strong></h3>
<p>NER is a subfield of <a title="Information Extraction NER" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Information_extraction" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Information Extraction</a> that deals with locating and classifying named entities into predefined categories like person names, organization, location, event, date, etc. from an unstructured document. NER is to an extent similar to Keyword Extraction except for the fact that the extracted keywords are put into already defined categories. This is indeed one step ahead of what we do with keyword extraction. There are built-in functions in Spacy to do this. We&rsquo;ll use a new excerpt from an article.</p>
<p style="text-align: center;"><img style="border-width: 0px; border-style: solid; width: 468px; height: 79px;" title="Named Entity Recognition NLP Technique" alt="Named Entity Recognition NLP Technique" data-src="https://daxg39y63pxwu.cloudfront.net/images/blog/10+NLP+Techniques+Every+Data+Scientist+Should+Learn/Named+Entity+Recognition+NLP+Technique.png"></p>
<p>SpaCy can easily extract entities from this in a line or two.</p>
<p style="text-align: center;"><img style="border-width: 0px; border-style: solid; width: 468px; height: 135px;" title="NER" alt="NER" data-src="https://daxg39y63pxwu.cloudfront.net/images/blog/10+NLP+Techniques+Every+Data+Scientist+Should+Learn/NER_NLP+Technique.png"></p>
<p>The&nbsp;predefined categories associated with extracted entities are self-explanatory except for:</p>
<ul><li>CARDINAL- which stands for a countable number.</li>
<li>GPE- stands for countries, cities, states.</li>
<li>NORP- is for Nationalities or religious or political groups.&nbsp;</li>
</ul><p>To learn more about these categories, you can refer to this <a title="SpaCy Entity Recognizer" href="https://spacy.io/api/entityrecognizer" target="_blank" rel="noopener">documentation</a>. We can also visualize the text with entities using displacy- a function provided by SpaCy.<br>&nbsp;</p>
<p style="text-align: center;"><img style="border-width: 0px; border-style: solid; width: 468px; height: 150px;" title="Named Entity Recognition NLP Python" alt="Named Entity Recognition NLP Python" data-src="https://daxg39y63pxwu.cloudfront.net/images/blog/10+NLP+Techniques+Every+Data+Scientist+Should+Learn/NLPTechnique_NERModel.png"></p>
<p>Here, we have used a predefined NER model but you can also train your own NER model from scratch. However, this is useful when the dataset is very domain-specific and SpaCy cannot find most entities in it.&nbsp;One of the examples where this usually happens is with the name of Indian cities and public figures- spacy isn&rsquo;t able to accurately tag them.</p>
